# Dr. Mohamed Mwamzandi: Carrying Bantu Languages into the Digital Age

## Overview

**Position:** Teaching Associate Professor, Department of African, African American, and Diaspora Studies, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill
**Field:** African linguistics, Swahili language pedagogy, computational linguistics
**Education:** PhD in Linguistics (University of Texas at Arlington), MA in Languages and Linguistics (Egerton University, Kenya)

---

In a department at one of America's premier public universities, a Kenyan linguist teaches students to speak Swahili while simultaneously building the computational tools that may help preserve the Bantu languages of East Africa — including, by extension, the Digo language from which he comes. Mohamed Mwamzandi's career represents a path less visible than political leadership but no less consequential for the future of languages like Chidigo: the path of academic scholarship applied to language preservation and technological adaptation.

## From Egerton to Chapel Hill

Mwamzandi's academic trajectory spans three continents. He earned his Bachelor of Education and Master of Arts in Languages and Linguistics at **Egerton University** in Kenya, then completed a Certificate in Africa Language Pedagogy at Indiana University's **National African Language Resource Center** before earning his PhD in Linguistics at the **University of Texas at Arlington**.

He joined the faculty at **UNC Chapel Hill**, where he teaches African linguistics and Swahili language and culture. His pedagogical philosophy centres on Swahili as a gateway: he emphasises that Swahili functions as a lingua franca across nine East and Central African nations and serves "as a gateway to learning the over 400 Bantu languages spoken in Africa." This framing positions Swahili not as a destination but as an entry point — a philosophy that implicitly validates the study and preservation of Bantu languages like Chidigo that exist downstream of Swahili in the regional linguistic hierarchy.

## Research: From Syntax to Security

Mwamzandi's research spans several domains of linguistics:

**Corpus linguistics and morpho-syntax:** His published work includes studies on Swahili conditional constructions and demonstrative positioning, using corpus-based methodologies to analyse grammatical patterns. These are technical contributions to Bantu linguistics, but they also build the kind of annotated language data that computational tools — dictionaries, translation systems, language-learning platforms — require to function.

**Computational linguistics:** His interest in computational approaches to African languages places him at the frontier of a field that will determine whether languages like Chidigo survive the digital transition. Languages that lack digital corpora, annotated texts, and computational models are increasingly excluded from the tools — search engines, AI assistants, educational software — through which the next generation encounters language. Mwamzandi's work on computational methods for Swahili creates models and methodologies that can, in principle, be extended to related Bantu languages.

**Counter-violent extremism:** In a more applied vein, Mwamzandi has researched "counter violent extremism efforts in Africa and how the use of local languages may help in the war against terror." This work connects language scholarship to security policy, arguing that effective communication in local languages — rather than top-down messaging in national languages — is essential for community engagement in affected regions, including the Kenya coast.

## Key Publications

- **"A Corpus Study of Swahili Conditional Constructions"** — Published in *Studies in African Linguistics*, this work uses corpus data to analyse how Swahili speakers construct conditional sentences, contributing to the grammatical description of the language.

- **Studies on Swahili demonstrative position** — Corpus-based analysis of how demonstratives function in natural Swahili speech.

- **Papers on Swahili reciprocals** — Exploring the morpho-syntactic patterns of reciprocal constructions.

- **Research on counter-terrorism applications of local language use** — Applied linguistics work on how indigenous African languages can serve security and peacebuilding objectives.

## Cross-Cultural Language Education

At UNC, Mwamzandi has participated in the **COIL (Collaborative Online International Learning)** programme, which connects students across institutions and countries for shared language-learning experiences. This kind of pedagogical innovation — using technology to create cross-cultural language encounters — models the kind of approach that could one day benefit smaller languages like Chidigo, connecting learners across the diaspora with speakers in Kwale and Tanga.

## Significance

Mohamed Mwamzandi does not, in his published work, focus specifically on the Digo language. But his significance for the Digo linguistic future lies in the infrastructure his work builds: the corpus methods, the computational frameworks, the pedagogical models, and the institutional presence of East African linguistics at a major research university. Every annotated Swahili corpus, every computational model of Bantu morphology, every student who learns that Africa's 400+ Bantu languages are worthy of serious study — these create the conditions under which a language like Chidigo might find its way into the digital world rather than being left behind.

In a community where the proverb warns that *"Mvula igodzwa na utseru"* — the rain is awaited by the one who has prepared — Mwamzandi's work is a form of preparation: building the tools, training the people, and establishing the intellectual foundations that will be needed when the moment comes to carry Chidigo fully into the 21st century.
