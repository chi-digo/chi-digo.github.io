# Prof. Hassan Mwakimako: Scholar of Islam on the Kenya Coast

## Overview

**Affiliation:** Associate Professor, Department of Philosophy and Religious Studies, Pwani University, Kilifi, Kenya
**Field:** Islamic Studies, with focus on the intersection of colonial/post-colonial state policy and Islam
**Role in Digo community:** Secretary of the Digo Ngambi (traditional leadership council)

---

In the academic study of Islam in East Africa, a small number of scholars have done the painstaking work of recovering the institutional history of Muslim communities on the Kenya coast — the kadhi courts, the mosques, the madrasas, and the political organisations through which coastal Muslims have negotiated their place in the Kenyan state. Hassan Mwakimako is among the most productive of them.

## Academic Career

Mwakimako holds a PhD and is Associate Professor at **Pwani University** in Kilifi, on the Kenya coast. His appointment at a coastal university is significant: much Kenyan academic research on Islam has been produced from Nairobi-based institutions, at some remove from the communities studied. Mwakimako works from within the region whose history he documents.

His research sits at the intersection of several fields: Islamic legal history, Christian-Muslim relations, colonial governance, and the politics of religious identity in post-colonial Kenya. He has published in peer-reviewed journals and contributed chapters to edited volumes published by Edinburgh University Press, Cambridge University Press, and Brill.

## Key Publications

Mwakimako's publication record spans more than a decade and includes:

- **"Kadhi Court and Appointment of Kadhi in Kenya Colony"** — An examination of how British colonial rule restructured Islamic judicial authority, creating formal kadhi courts that changed the way the *ulama* (Islamic scholars) operated on the coast.

- **"Islam, Politics, and Violence on the Kenya Coast"** — An analysis of the politicisation of Islam in the coastal region, a topic of acute relevance given the security challenges that have affected Mombasa and Kwale in recent decades.

- **"Christian–Muslim Relations in Kenya: A Catalogue of Events and Meanings"** — A systematic mapping of interfaith relations, examining historical patterns and contemporary flashpoints.

- **"Islam and Democracy: Debating Electoral Involvement on the Kenya Coast"** — An exploration of how coastal Muslims navigate the tension between democratic participation and Islamic political theology, noting that "many Muslims engage in democracy despite calls for electoral boycotts."

- **"The Supreme Council of Kenya Muslims (SUPKEM): Jostling for Representativeness"** — A study of the institutional politics of Muslim representation in Kenya.

- **"Preaching Politics: Islam and Christianity on the Kenya Coast"** (with Gregory Deacon and George Gona) — A comparative study of how the two faiths engage with public political life on the coast.

- Co-editor, **"Governance and Islam in East Africa: Muslims and the State"** (Edinburgh University Press) — A major volume examining Islamic governance across the region.

## The Ngambi Role

Beyond academia, Mwakimako serves as **Secretary** of the Digo Ngambi — the traditional leadership council revived in October 2023 to represent the Digo community's collective interests. In this role, he has been instrumental in articulating the intellectual case for the Ngambi's existence.

He explained to journalists that the *Ngambi* represents the traditional Digo leadership structure: "The Digo community had traditional rulers and leaders through the Ngambi," and the current initiative represents a "revival of the same for cultural revival." He has also framed the broader case for Digo institutional representation: "There is nothing wrong with the Digo having a traditional authority, just as is the case with other communities like the Kikuyu and the Luo, to ensure the protection and preservation of their traditions."

This dual role — academic authority and community institutional leadership — places Mwakimako in a position to bridge the gap between scholarly understanding of Digo history and the community's practical need for organised representation. His academic expertise in how colonial and post-colonial states have engaged with Muslim communities on the coast directly informs his work helping the Digo community navigate the same structures.

## Research Collaborators

Mwakimako's work connects him to a network of East African scholars including Gregory Deacon, Ngala Chome (Ghent University), and Hassan Ndzovu (Moi University). This network has collectively produced some of the most important recent scholarship on coastal Kenyan Islam, moving the field beyond the older generation of Western scholars (Sperling, Pouwels) toward locally grounded, coast-based analysis.

## Significance for Digo History

While Mwakimako's published work addresses Islam on the Kenya coast broadly rather than Digo history specifically, his institutional position and community role make him a crucial figure in the ongoing effort to document and advocate for Digo cultural identity. As a scholar who studies how Muslim communities on the coast have been governed, marginalised, and represented, and as a Digo community leader working to revive traditional governance structures, he embodies the intersection of knowledge and action that the Digo community needs at this moment in its history.
