# Sheikh Mwinyi Kombo: The Man Who Found the Mosque in the Forest

## Overview

**Era:** c. early 1700s
**Role:** Muslim scholar and restorer of the Kongo Mosque
**Region:** Diani, Kwale County, present-day Kenya

---

Somewhere around three hundred years ago, a Muslim scholar named Mwinyi Kombo received a revelation in his sleep. In the dream, he was commanded to search along the Diani coastline for a hidden mosque — a structure that no living person knew existed, swallowed by centuries of forest growth. What he found would become one of the most significant Islamic heritage sites in East Africa.

## The Dream and the Search

The details of the revelation remain sparse in the historical record, passed down through oral tradition rather than written chronicle. What is known is that Mwinyi Kombo took the dream seriously enough to undertake what sources describe as a "strenuous" search requiring "patience and tenacity." He and a small group of men pushed through dense coastal shrub and overgrown forest along the Diani shoreline, searching for a structure that might no longer exist.

They found it. Buried beneath decades — perhaps centuries — of vegetation, obscured by baobab trees and strangler figs, stood a coral stone mosque. Its walls were intact but its interior had become, in the words of one account, "a home for bats, rats and snakes." The building had been constructed in the 13th or 14th century by Arab or Persian merchants who traded along the Swahili coast. It had been built using *coral rag* — blocks of fossilised coral quarried from the reef — in a technique common to the medieval Swahili architecture found at Gedi, Kilwa, and other coastal ruins. But unlike those famous sites, this mosque had been forgotten entirely.

## The Restoration

Mwinyi Kombo led the rehabilitation of the structure. The mosque was cleared, its prayer hall restored, and worship resumed at the site for what may have been the first time in centuries. He renamed it the **Kongo Mosque** — *Msikiti wa Kongo* — in honour of **Swaddiq Kongo**, a devout Muslim believer said to be buried at the site. The name "Kongo" thus memorialises not the building's original founders but a figure associated with faith at that specific place, linking the mosque's identity to an individual rather than a dynasty or trading house.

The original structure had been known as the **Diani Persian Mosque**, reflecting the Persian Gulf connections of its builders. Its construction technique — coral stone walls, a mihrab (prayer niche) oriented toward Mecca, and a simple rectangular plan — places it within the broader tradition of early Swahili coast mosques. Archaeologists have dated it to between the 11th and 14th centuries, making it contemporaneous with the great mosques of Lamu and Kilwa.

## The Mosque's Significance

The Kongo Mosque today sits at the northern fringes of Diani Beach, a short distance from the tourist hotels and beach bars that dominate the modern coastline. In 1983, the National Museums of Kenya gazetted it as a **national monument**, recognising its archaeological and historical importance.

The mosque remains an active place of worship. Friday prayers are held regularly, and the site draws visitors — both Muslim pilgrims and tourists — who come to see one of the oldest standing mosques in East Africa. The coral stone walls, weathered but upright, still bear the marks of medieval craftsmanship: carved coral pilasters, a recessed mihrab, and the characteristic lime plaster of Swahili coast architecture.

In recent years, the mosque and its surrounding land have been at the centre of a legal dispute. The 4.5-acre plot on which it sits was valued at approximately KSh 1.4 billion, and the local Muslim community has fought to prevent its sale to private developers. The dispute has drawn national political attention, underscoring the tension between heritage preservation and coastal land speculation.

## Mwinyi Kombo's Place in Digo History

Sheikh Mwinyi Kombo's ethnic identity is not definitively recorded — he may have been Digo, Swahili, or of mixed heritage, as was common in the cosmopolitan coastal settlements of the era. What is certain is that his rediscovery of the Kongo Mosque represents a pivotal moment in the Islamic history of the Digo coast. By restoring a physical link to centuries of Muslim presence in Diani, he provided the Digo community with tangible evidence that Islam was not a foreign imposition but a faith with deep local roots.

The story of the architect and the first Muslims who built and worshipped at the original Diani Persian Mosque has, as one source notes, "sadly been lost in the sands of time." But because of Mwinyi Kombo's dream and his determination to act on it, the building itself survived — a coral monument to faith, persistence, and the power of a single person's conviction that what the ancestors built deserves to endure.
